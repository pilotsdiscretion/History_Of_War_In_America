Revolutionary War ⚑ Congress raised the Army, 1775

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

The war began on April 19, 1775 at Lexington and Concord, after a decade of escalating disputes between Britain and its thirteen colonies over taxation without representation (the Stamp Act, Townshend duties) and London's tightening control after the 1773 Boston Tea Party and the 1774 "Intolerable Acts." The Second Continental Congress created the Continental Army in June 1775 and declared independence on July 4, 1776. Americans fought to win independence from the British Crown and secure self-government.

Primary actors: Gen. George Washington (American, Commander-in-Chief); King George III & PM Lord North (Britain); British commanders Gen. William Howe and Gen. Charles Cornwallis; allies King Louis XVI of France, the Marquis de Lafayette and Comte de Rochambeau (France, allied 1778); Native nations fought on both sides (e.g., Iroquois under Joseph Brant with Britain).

The Course of the War

1775–1776 — New England & independence. The war opened with the siege of Boston and the bloody British victory at Bunker Hill; the British evacuated Boston in March 1776. After independence was declared, a disastrous New York campaign nearly destroyed Washington's army — saved only by his daring Christmas crossing of the Delaware to win at Trenton and Princeton.

1777–1778 — Saratoga & the French alliance. Britain took Philadelphia, but the surrender of an entire British army at Saratoga (October 1777) was the war's turning point: it convinced France to ally openly in February 1778. The army survived the brutal winter at Valley Forge, where Baron von Steuben forged it into a disciplined force. Spain (1779) and the Dutch soon joined against Britain, widening the conflict into a global war.

1778–1781 — the Southern strategy & Yorktown. Britain shifted south, taking Savannah and Charleston (the worst American defeat of the war). Nathanael Greene's war of attrition — Cowpens and Guilford Courthouse — bled Cornwallis, who withdrew to Virginia. With Admiral de Grasse's French fleet controlling the Chesapeake, Washington and Rochambeau trapped Cornwallis at Yorktown in October 1781.

The home front. Colonists split roughly into Patriots, Loyalists, and the neutral; some 60,000 Loyalists ultimately fled. Both sides recruited the enslaved — Britain's Dunmore Proclamation promised freedom, while ~5,000 African Americans served the Patriot cause. Rampant inflation of "Continental" paper money (hence "not worth a Continental") made war finance a constant crisis.

⚠ War Crimes & Atrocities

  • British prison ships: an estimated 8,000–11,500 American POWs died of disease and starvation aboard hulks like HMS Jersey in Wallabout Bay — more than died in all battles combined.
  • Waxhaws (1780): "Tarleton's quarter" — Continental troops reportedly killed after surrendering, in the Carolinas.
  • Frontier raids & reprisals: massacres on both sides involving Loyalist and Native combatants (e.g., Cherry Valley; the Gnadenhütten massacre of Christian Delaware, 1782).

Outcome & Lasting Impact

Independence won. The Treaty of Paris (1783) recognized U.S. independence and ceded British land east of the Mississippi, roughly doubling the new nation's territory. Washington's voluntary resignation of his commission set the bedrock American principle of civilian control of the military.

From a weak confederation to the Constitution. The war was nearly lost under the feeble Articles of Confederation, which left Congress unable to tax or pay its soldiers (fueling the 1783 Newburgh near-mutiny and 1786 Shays's Rebellion). Those failures drove the Constitutional Convention of 1787 and a federal government empowered to tax, raise armies, and honor its debts.

Debt, finance & unfinished business. The ~$75M war debt gave rise to Alexander Hamilton's assumption plan, national bank, and bond market. The Declaration's claim that "all men are created equal" stood against the reality of slavery — a contradiction left unresolved until the Civil War. Abroad, the Revolution helped inspire the French Revolution and Latin American independence.

Northwest Indian War ⚑ Congress funded the Legion, 1792

1785–1795 · The new republic's first major war — for the Ohio Country.

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

The 1783 Treaty of Paris handed the Ohio Country (the Northwest Territory) to the United States, but the Native nations living there were never party to the treaty and resisted American surveyors and settlers. A confederacy of Western tribes — quietly backed by Britain, which still garrisoned forts on U.S. soil — fought to hold the Ohio River as the boundary. President Washington went to war to enforce the U.S. claim to the territory and open it to settlement; an early disaster (St. Clair's Defeat, 1791) nearly destroyed the army.

Primary actors: President George Washington (U.S.); U.S. commanders Gen. Josiah Harmar, Gen. Arthur St. Clair, and Gen. "Mad" Anthony Wayne; confederacy leaders Little Turtle (Miami) and Blue Jacket (Shawnee); Great Britain (supplying the confederacy from frontier forts).

Why It Mattered

Defeat → reform: Congress replaced the militia model with a professional Legion

St. Clair's Defeat consumed roughly a quarter of federal military spending and nearly the whole army. Congress responded by funding Anthony Wayne's Legion of the United States, which won decisively at Fallen Timbers (1794). The war set the precedent of a standing professional army and of Native land cession by treaty after conquest.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

The Treaty of Greenville (1795) forced the confederacy to cede most of present-day Ohio and part of Indiana, opening the Northwest to settlement (Ohio became a state in 1803). The companion Jay Treaty (1794) finally pried the British out of their frontier forts by 1796.

It built the U.S. Army. St. Clair's catastrophe triggered the first congressional investigation of the executive branch and convinced Washington to fund a professional force — Anthony Wayne's Legion of the United States, ancestor of the modern Army — over Americans' deep distrust of standing armies.

A template repeated. The cycle of war, conquest, and a land-cession treaty became the model for U.S. continental expansion for the next century. The Greenville line was almost immediately overrun by settlers, fueling Tecumseh's pan-Indian resistance and the War of 1812 in the West.

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

After the U.S. signed the Jay Treaty with Britain (1794) and stopped repaying debts to the deposed French monarchy, Revolutionary France began seizing American merchant ships trading with Britain. The XYZ Affair (1797–98) — in which French agents demanded bribes before they would even negotiate — inflamed American opinion. With no declaration of war, Congress authorized the Navy to fight. The U.S. fought to protect its merchant shipping and defend its neutrality.

Primary actors: President John Adams (U.S.); Benjamin Stoddert (first U.S. Secretary of the Navy); Commodore Thomas Truxtun (USS Constellation); the French Directory and foreign minister Talleyrand with agents "X, Y, Z" (France).

Birth of the U.S. Navy

The Quasi-War created a permanent naval establishment

Congress created the Department of the Navy (1798), re-established the Marine Corps, and put the "six original frigates" to sea. It set the precedent — repeated for two centuries — that the President could wage limited, undeclared war when authorized by statute rather than a formal declaration.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

The Convention of 1800 (Treaty of Mortefontaine) peacefully ended the fighting and dissolved the 1778 Franco-American alliance — freeing the U.S. from "entangling alliances" and clearing the way for the 1803 Louisiana Purchase from Napoleon.

A permanent Navy. The war established the Department of the Navy and a standing fleet of heavy frigates and revived the Marine Corps — the force that would soon fight the Barbary pirates and the War of 1812.

Precedent & politics. It set the enduring precedent of limited, undeclared war authorized by statute rather than formal declaration. At home, war fever produced the Alien and Sedition Acts (1798), whose backlash helped elect Jefferson in 1800 and strengthened the tradition against punishing political speech.

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

The Barbary states of North Africa — Tripoli, Tunis, and Algiers, nominally under the Ottoman Empire — financed themselves by seizing Mediterranean shipping and enslaving captured sailors unless paid tribute. The young U.S. had been paying. When Yusuf Karamanli, Pasha of Tripoli, demanded more and declared war in 1801, President Thomas Jefferson refused and sent the Navy. The U.S. fought to end tribute and protect American commerce and seamen.

Primary actors: President Thomas Jefferson (U.S.); Commodore Edward Preble, Lt. Stephen Decatur, and diplomat-soldier William Eaton (U.S.); Yusuf Karamanli, Pasha of Tripoli (Barbary North Africa).

War Contractors & Profiteers

The "six original frigates" authorized by the Naval Act of 1794 (USS Constitution, Constellation, United States, etc.), designed by Joshua Humphreys and built in private East Coast shipyards, were the principal "defense contract" of the era. No public defense companies or stocks existed.

Tribute/ransom figures are approximate and vary by source; the ~$1M cumulative tribute represented a large share of early federal revenue. From the Naval History & Heritage Command and standard histories.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

The 1805 treaty ended tribute to Tripoli (though a $60,000 ransom was still paid for the Philadelphia's crew) and freed the captives. It was America's first war fought overseas and an early test of presidential war-making — Jefferson, a limited-government skeptic, nonetheless projected naval power across the Atlantic.

It forged the Navy and Marine Corps. The war made a national hero of Stephen Decatur and produced enduring Marine Corps lore — the march on Derna gave the Marines' Hymn its line "to the shores of Tripoli" and the officers' Mameluke sword — while validating the heavy-frigate fleet built in the 1790s.

Unfinished business. Tribute to Algiers continued and the Barbary threat revived during the War of 1812, to be settled only by the Second Barbary War of 1815. The conflict established a lasting American principle: defend free commerce by force rather than pay extortion.

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

Britain, locked in war with Napoleonic France, blockaded Europe and seized American ships and cargoes, and "impressed" thousands of U.S. sailors into the Royal Navy. Britain also armed Native resistance on the frontier, where "War Hawks" in Congress eyed British Canada. President James Madison asked Congress for war, and on June 18, 1812 it issued the first formal U.S. declaration of war. The U.S. fought over maritime rights (impressment, neutral trade) and frontier security.

Primary actors: President James Madison (U.S.); War Hawks Henry Clay & John C. Calhoun (U.S. Congress); the Prince Regent & Royal Navy (Britain); Tecumseh (Shawnee), leader of a Native confederacy allied with Britain; U.S. commanders Gen. Andrew Jackson and Cdre. Oliver Hazard Perry.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

A draw that felt like a win. The Treaty of Ghent (December 1814) restored the prewar status quo — no territory changed hands and impressment went unmentioned — yet Andrew Jackson's lopsided victory at New Orleans (fought after the treaty was signed) convinced Americans they had won a "Second War of Independence," igniting a surge of nationalism and the "Era of Good Feelings."

Political & economic realignment. The antiwar Federalist Party, tarnished by the secession-tinged Hartford Convention, collapsed. The British blockade had spurred domestic manufacturing, and the war's strains led to the Second Bank of the United States (1816) and protective tariffs.

Catastrophe for Native nations. The death of Tecumseh (1813) shattered his confederacy and ended large-scale Native resistance east of the Mississippi, opening the interior to settlement and removal. The war also propelled Andrew Jackson toward the presidency and gave the nation Francis Scott Key's "Star-Spangled Banner," made the national anthem in 1931.

Why It Mattered

The campaign — over in weeks — ended three decades of American tribute payments to North Africa and announced that the post-1812 United States would protect its commerce by force. It also cemented the precedent of congressional authorization short of a formal declaration for a foreign war.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

Outcome & Lasting Impact

Conquest & confinement. Background & Why the U.S. Fought

The war's cause was slavery and its expansion. After Abraham Lincoln's 1860 election, eleven slave states seceded and formed the Confederacy to preserve slavery; they fired on Fort Sumter in April 1861. The U.S. government fought first to preserve the Union and, from 1863 (Emancipation Proclamation), explicitly to end slavery. It was the only U.S. war fought on home soil between Americans, and the first with a national draft on both sides.

Primary actors: President Abraham Lincoln (United States / Union) and Generals Ulysses S. Grant & William T. Sherman; President Jefferson Davis (Confederate States) and Generals Robert E. Lee & "Stonewall" Jackson.

The Course of the War

1861 — secession & Sumter. After Lincoln's election seven Deep South states seceded; the firing on Fort Sumter (April 1861) brought four more. The Union's hope of a quick victory died at First Bull Run, and both sides raised vast volunteer armies.

1862 — bloody stalemate & emancipation. Shiloh, the Seven Days, and Antietam (the bloodiest single day in U.S. history) showed the war would be long and total. Antietam let Lincoln issue the Emancipation Proclamation, recasting the war as a fight to end slavery and discouraging European recognition of the Confederacy.

1863 — the turning points. Gettysburg (Lee's failed invasion of the North) and Vicksburg (which split the Confederacy along the Mississippi) fell within a day of each other in July. Some 180,000 Black soldiers (USCT) ultimately joined the Union ranks.

1864–65 — attrition & total war. Grant's relentless Overland Campaign pinned Lee at Petersburg while Sherman took Atlanta — securing Lincoln's reelection — and marched through Georgia waging "hard war." Richmond fell and Lee surrendered at Appomattox in April 1865.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

Union and emancipation. The war preserved the United States as one nation and destroyed slavery: the 13th Amendment (1865) abolished it, freeing some 4 million people. The 14th (1868) guaranteed citizenship and equal protection and the 15th (1870) barred racial bars to voting — the constitutional foundation of modern civil rights.

Reconstruction & its betrayal. Lincoln's assassination days after Appomattox left Reconstruction to bitter struggle; its gains were rolled back after the disputed 1876 election, and Jim Crow segregation, disenfranchisement, and the "Lost Cause" myth held for nearly a century until the Civil Rights Movement.

A transformed nation. The deadliest war in U.S. history (~620,000–750,000 dead) settled that the Union was perpetual and federal power supreme — Americans even began saying "the United States is" rather than "are." It sped industrialization, created the first national income tax and national banking, and gave rise to national cemeteries, veterans' pensions, and Memorial Day.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

An overseas empire. The imperial debate. Annexation split the country — the Anti-Imperialist League (Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie) opposed ruling foreign peoples — and led directly to the brutal Philippine–American War. Puerto Rico and Guam remain U.S. territories today.

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

The U.S. stayed neutral for nearly three years of European slaughter, but Germany's unrestricted submarine warfare (sinking the Lusitania in 1915 and U.S. merchant ships) and the Zimmermann Telegram — Germany's secret offer to help Mexico retake the U.S. Southwest — pushed opinion to war. Primary actors: President Woodrow Wilson (U.S.) and Gen. John J. Pershing (American Expeditionary Forces); Kaiser Wilhelm II and the German High Command (Germany); Allied leaders of Britain and France.

The Course of the War (the American role)

Mobilizing a nation. The U.S. entered in April 1917 with a tiny army; the Selective Service Act and a vast war economy raised, trained, and shipped some 2 million men of Pershing's American Expeditionary Forces to France within 18 months.

Turning the tide (1918). Fresh U.S. troops helped halt Germany's last Spring Offensive; Americans were blooded at Cantigny and Belleau Wood, then drove forward at St. Mihiel and in the massive Meuse–Argonne Offensive (~1.2 million U.S. troops; ~26,000 American dead) that helped break the German line.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

Disillusionment. Staggering casualties for an ambiguous peace bred deep anti-war isolationism; the 1930s "merchants of death" hearings and Neutrality Acts grew directly out of WWI — a mood the U.S. would not shed until Pearl Harbor.

Background & Why the U.S. Fought

When communist North Korea invaded the U.S.-aligned South in June 1950, President Harry Truman committed U.S. forces under a UN Security Council resolution to "contain" communism — but he never asked Congress to declare war, calling it a "police action." The Course of the War

Invasion & near-defeat (1950). North Korea's surprise attack drove the South to the Pusan Perimeter; MacArthur's daring Inchon landing reversed the war and UN forces pushed toward the Chinese border.

China intervenes (1950–51). Hundreds of thousands of Chinese troops poured in, hurling the UN back below Seoul before the line stabilized near the 38th parallel. Truman relieved MacArthur in 1951 for publicly pushing to widen the war — a landmark assertion of civilian control.

Stalemate & armistice (1951–53). Two years of bloody hill fights and POW disputes ended in the July 27, 1953 armistice — a cease-fire, not a peace treaty.

Outcome & Lasting Impact

A divided peninsula, still at war. The armistice restored the prewar border at the DMZ; no peace treaty was ever signed, so the Koreas remain technically at war. The toll was staggering — ~36,500 U.S. dead and an estimated 2–3 million Koreans plus hundreds of thousands of Chinese.

The template for the Cold War. Korea set the pattern of limited war to contain — not roll back — communism, tripled U.S. defense spending (NSC-68), and locked in a permanent U.S. military presence in Asia. It was the first war fought under the UN flag and the first by a desegregated U.S. military.

A frozen legacy. The "Forgotten War" created the two Koreas of today — a prosperous democratic South and a nuclear-armed North — and a direct line to the strategy the U.S. carried into Vietnam.

Conscription Powered the War

~1.9M Americans were drafted for Vietnam; the draft drove the antiwar movement

About 1.9 million men were drafted (of ~8.7M who served in the era). A 1969 lottery and widespread deferments fueled charges of class inequity; draft-card burning became the icon of protest. The draft ended in 1973 — see the Conscription & the Draft tab.

⚠ War Crimes & Atrocities

  • My Lai Massacre (Mar 1968): U.S. soldiers killed 347–504 unarmed villagers; only Lt. William Calley was convicted (paroled after ~3½ years). Helicopter crewman Hugh Thompson landed to stop the killing.
  • Free-fire zones & "body counts": pressure to maximize enemy counts produced many civilian deaths; Operation Speedy Express (1968–69) drew allegations of thousands of civilian killings.
  • Tiger Force & the Phoenix Program: a recon platoon's 1967 killing spree, and the CIA-run Phoenix Program that "neutralized" tens of thousands of suspected VC amid torture and assassinations.
  • Agent Orange & napalm: ~20 million gallons of herbicide (Operation Ranch Hand) left lasting cancers and birth defects; napalm burned civilians (the "Napalm Girl" photo).
  • North Vietnamese / Viet Cong atrocities: the Huế Massacre (1968) — communist forces executed an estimated 2,800–6,000 civilians — plus systematic assassination of village officials and terror in the countryside.

⚔️ Viet Cong & NVA Tactics

The communists rarely fought on American terms. The Viet Cong waged a guerrilla war — ambushes, hit-and-run raids, and booby traps (punji stakes, tripwires, mines) that caused a large share of U.S. casualties — then melted back into the population. An elaborate tunnel network (the Củ Chi tunnels) and the Ho Chi Minh Trail through Laos and Cambodia kept them supplied and hidden. They fought at night, "hugged the belt" (closing to point-blank range to neutralize U.S. airpower and artillery), used sappers against bases, and assassinated officials to control villages. The 1968 Tet Offensive — 100+ coordinated attacks — was a tactical defeat but a strategic shock. Their real target was the attrition of American will, not ground.

✊ Opposition at Home — the Antiwar Movement

Vietnam produced the largest antiwar movement in U.S. history. It grew from campus teach-ins (1965) and draft-card burnings to the March on the Pentagon (1967), the chaotic 1968 Democratic Convention, and the Moratorium protests of late 1969 (millions nationwide; ~500,000 in Washington). It turned deadly at Kent State (May 4, 1970 — National Guardsmen killed four students) and Jackson State (two killed). Returning soldiers joined via Vietnam Veterans Against the War and the 1971 "Winter Soldier" hearings; the leaked Pentagon Papers (1971) exposed years of official deception. Opinion flipped — in 1965 just 24% called the war a mistake; by 1971, 60% did, and 74% by 1990.

🎗️ The War's Legacy — the VA, PTSD & Veterans

No U.S. war did more to change how the nation treats its veterans' minds. Troops came home to a divided country with little support, and many struggled with what was first called "post-Vietnam syndrome." Veterans' "rap groups" and advocacy drove the formal recognition of Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder — added to the DSM-III in 1980. The landmark National Vietnam Veterans Readjustment Study found 15.2% of male Vietnam veterans had PTSD in the late 1980s and 30.9% had suffered it at some point, rising sharply with combat exposure.

The war reshaped the VA: Congress created the nationwide Vet Center readjustment-counseling program in 1979, and the Agent Orange Act (1991) made many cancers presumptively service-connected. Vietnam veterans also became the face of veteran homelessness: in late-1980s VA studies roughly half of all homeless veterans were Vietnam-era (vs. ~29% of all veterans), and young veterans were several times more likely to be homeless than non-veterans — driven by PTSD, substance abuse, and thin readjustment support.

Note: the widely repeated claim that more Vietnam veterans later died by suicide than were killed in the war is not supported by evidence and is regarded as a myth — though suicide and substance abuse were, and remain, serious problems. source ↗

✊ Opposition at Home — and the Rally Effect

The Gulf War was preceded by sharp debate: Congress's authorization was the closest war vote since 1812 (Senate 52–47), and a large antiwar movement — echoing Vietnam — marched under "No Blood for Oil," with about 75,000 demonstrators filling Washington on Jan 26, 1991 and protests in cities nationwide. It is the textbook case of "rally round the flag."

🎗️ Legacy — Gulf War Illness

Despite a low combat toll, an estimated 25–32% of the ~700,000 U.S. Gulf War veterans developed Gulf War Illness — a chronic multi-symptom disorder (fatigue, pain, cognitive and gastrointestinal problems) linked to exposures such as pesticides, anti-nerve-agent pills, oil-fire smoke, and depleted uranium. Background & Why the U.S. Fought

After the September 11, 2001 attacks by al-Qaeda — sheltered by Afghanistan's Taliban — Congress passed the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force, and the U.S. invaded to destroy al-Qaeda and topple the Taliban. The war to deny terrorists a haven became a 20-year nation-building effort. The U.S. withdrew in August 2021 and the Taliban swiftly returned to power.

Primary actors: Presidents Bush, Obama, Trump & Biden (U.S.); Osama bin Laden (al-Qaeda); Mullah Omar & the Taliban.

How the War Unfolded (2001–2021)

The invasion (2001). On October 7, 2001, Operation Enduring Freedom opened with U.S. airstrikes and CIA/special-forces teams partnering with the Afghan Northern Alliance. Kabul fell in November; at Tora Bora that December, Osama bin Laden slipped across the border into Pakistan. The Bonn Agreement installed Hamid Karzai as interim leader of a new Afghan government.

Stalemate & surge (2003–2014). As resources shifted to Iraq, the Taliban regrouped into a stubborn insurgency. President Obama ordered a surge that peaked near 100,000 U.S. troops in 2010–11 (alongside ~40,000 NATO/ISAF). Bin Laden was killed on May 2, 2011 in Abbottabad, Pakistan; the U.S. formally ended its "combat mission" in December 2014, shifting to the train-and-advise mission Resolute Support — but the fighting never stopped.

The deal & the end (2020–2021). On February 29, 2020, the Trump administration signed the Doha Agreement with the Taliban (negotiated by Zalmay Khalilzad and Mullah Baradar), setting a withdrawal timeline and sidelining the elected Afghan government. President Biden ordered a full pullout in April 2021. The Taliban overran the country within months and took Kabul on August 15, 2021.

🛬 The Withdrawal & the Fall of Kabul (2021)

The collapse. As U.S. forces departed under the Doha timeline, the Taliban overran provincial capitals in days. On August 15, 2021 they entered Kabul unopposed; President Ashraf Ghani fled the country, and the U.S.-built Afghan security forces — funded at roughly $90 billion over two decades — dissolved almost overnight.

The airlift. The U.S. raced to evacuate citizens and at-risk Afghans through Hamid Karzai International Airport, airlifting about 124,000 people in two weeks — one of the largest noncombatant evacuations in history — amid desperate crowds.

Falling from the sky. On August 16, Afghans mobbed a departing U.S. Air Force C-17, clinging to the fuselage and landing gear; video showed two or three people falling to their deaths as the jet climbed, and human remains were later found in a wheel well. (Air Force investigators cleared the crew of wrongdoing in 2022.)

Abbey Gate. On August 26, an ISIS-K suicide bomber struck the airport's Abbey Gate, killing 13 U.S. service members (11 Marines, a Navy corpsman, and a soldier) and about 170 Afghans — the deadliest day for U.S. troops in Afghanistan in a decade.

The "righteous strike" that wasn't. On August 29, a U.S. drone strike the Pentagon first called "righteous" instead killed 10 civilians, including aid worker Zemari Ahmadi and seven children. The military later admitted it was a tragic mistake; no one was disciplined, and condolence payments were offered.

🪖 What Was Left Behind

Weapons & equipment. Because the Afghan forces collapsed so quickly, an estimated $7.1 billion of U.S.-furnished materiel fell into Taliban hands — roughly 78 aircraft (~$923M), 316,260 small arms (~$512M), more than 40,000 vehicles, ~9,500 air-to-ground munitions, and nearly all the night-vision, surveillance, communications and biometric gear. A U.S. watchdog (SIGAR) later found this equipment formed "the core of the Taliban security apparatus."

The dogs. Viral photos of crated dogs at the airport spurred outrage that the U.S. had abandoned its military working dogs. The Defense Department said no U.S. military working dogs were left behind — all left with their handlers — and that the crated animals were shelter dogs in the care of Kabul Small Animal Rescue. But an estimated ~150 "contract working dogs" (trained by private firms, not government MWDs) were reportedly stranded when a chartered rescue flight never arrived, drawing condemnation from groups such as American Humane. source ↗

Conscription & the Draft

How the United States forced citizens into its wars — from Civil War riots to the all-volunteer force.

Background

For most of its history the U.S. relied on volunteers and state militias, but its largest wars required conscription — a compulsory draft. Both sides drafted in the Civil War; the World Wars and the Cold War institutionalized the Selective Service. The draft's inequities — commutation fees, deferments, and lotteries — repeatedly sparked unrest, from the 1863 New York Draft Riots to the Vietnam-era resistance. The draft ended in 1973, creating today's all-volunteer force, though men 18–25 must still register.

Inequity & Resistance

The Civil War let draftees buy out for $300 or hire a substitute ("rich man's war, poor man's fight"); the Confederacy's "Twenty Negro Law" exempted big slaveholders. The Vietnam draft's student and occupational deferments favored the well-off until a 1969 lottery; draft-card burning became the symbol of the antiwar movement. These inequities were a principal reason the nation moved to an all-volunteer force in 1973.

Legacy & Why It Matters Today

The all-volunteer force. The draft ended in 1973 after Vietnam, replaced by the all-volunteer force. Conscription's inequities — Civil War commutation fees, Vietnam-era student deferments — convinced leaders a volunteer military would be both fairer and more professional.

Still on the books. Men aged 18–25 must still register with the Selective Service, and Congress could revive a draft in a crisis. Whether women must register, and whether the nation should return to conscription, remain live legal and political questions.

The civil–military gap. With well under 1% of Americans now serving, critics warn of a widening distance between the military and the society it defends — and of how an all-volunteer force makes it easier to wage long wars with little shared public sacrifice.

The Constitution gives Congress the power "to declare War," but Congress has formally declared war only five times (covering 11 declarations against individual nations). The 1973 War Powers Resolution — passed over Nixon's veto — tried to rein in presidential war-making, but Presidents of both parties have largely sidestepped it (Kosovo 1999, Libya 2011).

🇮🇷 Iranian Drones & the Shahed Revolution

From the Iran–Iraq War to a drone superpower. Iran began building drones during the 1980s Iran–Iraq War; its Mohajer and Ababil families were among the world's first battlefield UAVs. Cut off from Western arms by sanctions, Tehran turned drones into a national specialty — fielding the jet-powered Karrar (2010) and the Reaper-like Shahed-129 — and became a major exporter to Russia and to proxies such as the Houthis and Hezbollah.

Reverse-engineering America. In December 2011 Iran brought down a U.S. RQ-170 Sentinel stealth drone nearly intact and spent two years copying it, producing the Saegheh and Shahed-171/191. Iran's program was built in part on cloned American technology.

How the Shahed-136 works. The Shahed-136 (Russia's Geran-2) is a loitering munition — a "one-way attack" or kamikaze drone nicknamed the "poor man's cruise missile." A delta-wing airframe (~3.5 m, ~200 kg) is boosted off a rack by a jettisoned rocket, then cruises on a cheap ~50-hp moped-style piston engine (its telltale buzz) along a pre-programmed GPS route before diving onto its target and detonating. It is slow (~185 km/h) but ranges up to ~2,500 km, carries a ~50 kg warhead (up to ~90 kg in Russian variants), and is fired in swarms to saturate air defenses.

The cost that changes war. Each Shahed costs only about $20,000–$50,000, yet forces defenders to expend interceptor missiles worth hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars apiece — an "exchange-rate" crisis for air defense. Russia now mass-produces them from Iranian blueprints and launches them by the hundreds in a single night.

Full circle — America clones the Shahed. In 2026 the story came full circle: U.S. contractor SpektreWorks built the LUCAS (FLM-136) — a one-way attack drone reverse-engineered from the Shahed-136, slightly smaller and costing about $35,000 (versus a ~$30 million Reaper). CENTCOM says LUCAS saw its first combat use in the February 2026 strikes on Iran — giving Tehran "a taste of its own medicine" — and the Pentagon has put roughly $1 billion behind cheap, mass-producible attack drones. source ↗

Conclusion

Two and a half centuries of American war — what the numbers reveal.

The Through-Lines

From 1775 to today, roughly 1.3 million Americans have died in the nation's wars, and U.S. military action has cost trillions of dollars. But several deeper shifts stand out: who dies, who decides, who serves, who profits, and how the fighting itself is done.

The Human Cost

The Civil War remains the deadliest U.S. war (~620,000–750,000). Before WWII, disease killed more soldiers than combat in nearly every war. And in most modern conflicts, enemy and civilian deaths dwarfed U.S. losses — by 10× to 100× in Korea, Vietnam, Iraq, and the Philippines.

Who Decides

Congress has formally declared war only five times, last in 1942. Since 1950, major wars have been fought under AUMFs or presidential authority — Korea, Vietnam, Kosovo, Libya — steadily shifting war power from Congress to the President.

Who Serves

The nation moved from militias to mass conscription (peaking at ~10M drafted in WWII) and, since 1973, to an all-volunteer force — concentrating the burden of war on a small professional minority.

Who Profits

From the Revolution's privateers and the Civil War's "shoddy" to WWI's "merchants of death," WWII's "Arsenal of Democracy," and Iraq's Halliburton/KBR, war has always made fortunes. Today's primes — RTX, Lockheed, Boeing — are valued in the hundreds of billions.

Manned to Unmanned

Warfare has moved from massed infantry toward precision and unmanned systems — from the first armed Predator strike in 2001 to thousands of drone strikes since, waging lethal war with no Americans at risk.

The Cost in Dollars

World War II was the costliest single war (~$4.1T in 2011 dollars). The post-9/11 wars total ~$8 trillion once veterans' care and interest are counted — much of it borrowed.

Why It Matters Going Forward

The arc of American war runs from citizen-soldiers fighting for survival to a global superpower that can wage lethal war by drone and proxy with few of its own troops at risk. Each war reshaped the nation: independence and the Constitution; emancipation and a stronger Union; the World Wars that made the U.S. a superpower; and the long, inconclusive "forever wars" after 9/11.

Three trends now define the future of American conflict: war power concentrated in the presidency (Congress has not declared war since 1941, yet force is used constantly under aging AUMFs); an all-volunteer, contractor-heavy force that lets the country fight long wars without broadly shared sacrifice; and unmanned, increasingly autonomous weapons that keep lowering the threshold for using force. Understanding who dies, who decides, who serves, and who profits is the key to judging the wars America may fight next.

🕊️ 2026 Ceasefire & the Strait of Hormuz (status as of June 19, 2026)

After U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran began on Feb 28, 2026, Iran retaliated against Israel and U.S. bases and closed the Strait of Hormuz (early March), attacking shipping and spiking world oil prices. A Pakistan-mediated ceasefire took hold on April 7–8, 2026 — a two-week truce with "safe passage" through the strait coordinated with Iran's armed forces — but it repeatedly frayed: President Trump accused Iran of failing to fully reopen the strait, and Israel and Iran traded heavy strikes again in early June. On June 14, 2026, mediators announced a Memorandum of Understanding, set to be signed June 19, 2026, intended to formally end the war within 60 days.

The Strait of Hormuz: roughly one-fifth of the world's oil (and a large share of its LNG) transits this 21-mile-wide chokepoint between Iran and Oman. Iran's closure triggered a global energy shock; Brent crude fell back toward ~$92 a barrel after the April truce. The deal requires Iran to immediately reopen the strait and join a regional framework guaranteeing safe navigation — though Iran's proposed tolls on transits (which U.S. officials called a deal-breaker) remain a sticking point.

Reported concessions / terms: Iran to reopen Hormuz and restore oil flows; Iran to halt its alleged pursuit of nuclear weapons as part of a final deal; the U.S. to ease sanctions in exchange for Iranian concessions; reconstruction and verification still to be negotiated. Background & Why the U.S. Fought

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on Feb 24, 2022 — following its 2014 seizure of Crimea and the war in the Donbas — drew an unprecedented U.S. response. Rather than send troops, the U.S. became Ukraine's largest backer, arming and funding it to resist Russia. It is the defining proxy war of the era: U.S.-supplied weapons against the Russian military, with no direct U.S. combat. By 2026 the war had become a grinding, high-casualty stalemate with no durable ceasefire.

Primary actors: Presidents Joe Biden then Donald Trump (U.S.); President Volodymyr Zelensky (Ukraine); President Vladimir Putin (Russia).

🛩️ FPV Drones & the Drone War

A new kind of war. The Russia–Ukraine war is the first major conflict dominated by cheap first-person-view (FPV) drones — racing quadcopters carrying an explosive charge, flown into a target by a pilot watching a live feed through goggles. Costing as little as $400–$500, an FPV drone can destroy a $4.5 million tank, upending the economics of war.

Industrial scale. Both sides now build drones by the million. Ukraine made ~2.2 million UAVs in 2024 (about 1.5M of them FPV) and contracted for roughly 4.5 million FPV drones in 2025, with capacity reportedly exceeding 8 million a year by 2026 — simple-FPV output jumped from ~20,000 to ~200,000 per month. Russia, which built ~1.4M drones in 2024, planned ~2 million FPVs (3–4M drones in total) for 2025.

The decisive weapon. Ukraine's National Security and Defense Council says FPV drones now inflict roughly 60% of Russia's equipment losses, and unmanned systems account for an estimated 60–80% of all battlefield casualties — more than artillery. Ukraine's drone units report striking over 350,000 Russian targets.

The jamming arms race. Because radio-controlled drones can be defeated by electronic warfare, both sides turned to fiber-optic FPV drones that unspool a hair-thin cable as they fly — immune to jamming, with crystal-clear video and ranges past 30 km (a Ukrainian winged FPV struck 102 km behind the line in 2026). The result is a drone-dominated "kill zone" reaching ~10–15 km on either side of the front — widening toward 40 km — where almost nothing can move in the open.

The U.S. is watching. Ukraine's drone revolution reshaped Pentagon thinking: the 2023 Replicator initiative aimed to field thousands of cheap, "attritable" autonomous drones (though it has lagged its targets), and the lessons of FPV warfare now drive U.S. and NATO procurement. source ↗

Outcome & Lasting Impact

A proxy war, still unresolved. The U.S. has committed roughly $175 billion to help Ukraine resist Russia's 2022 invasion without deploying U.S. combat troops — arming a partner against a nuclear-armed great power while trying to avoid direct war. As of 2026 the outcome remains undecided.

A revitalized West. The invasion reenergized NATO — bringing in Finland and Sweden — and pushed Europe to rearm, even as it drained U.S. and allied weapons stockpiles and exposed gaps in the defense industrial base.

A new era of competition. The largest land war in Europe since 1945 hardened the U.S.–Russia confrontation, drew Russia closer to China and Iran, and reopened a domestic debate over the costs and risks of open-ended support — hallmarks of renewed great-power rivalry.

Private Military Contractors ⚑ no single authorizing vote — funded via defense appropriations

1990s–present · the privatization of American war, from Baghdad to New Orleans.

Background — The Privatization of War

After the Cold War the U.S. military shrank, but its missions did not. To fill the gap — and to wage the post-9/11 wars without reinstating the draft — Washington turned to private military and security companies (PMCs/PSCs) on an unprecedented scale. Contractors cooked meals, built bases, drove convoys, guarded diplomats, trained foreign police, interrogated prisoners, and serviced drones. The Watson Institute estimates that one-third to one-half of the more than $14 trillion in post-9/11 Pentagon spending flowed to contractors. The model promised flexibility, surge capacity, and political deniability — fewer flag-draped coffins of uniformed troops — but it also pushed lethal force outside the normal chains of military law and public accountability.

Primary actors: Erik Prince (former U.S. Navy SEAL; founded Blackwater, 1996); Blackwater / Xe / Academi (Moyock, North Carolina); Triple Canopy (founded 2003 by U.S. Army Special Forces veterans); DynCorp International (police-training & aviation); and Constellis (the 2014 Academi–Triple Canopy merger). Oversight ran through the U.S. State Department (Bureau of Diplomatic Security) and the Department of Defense.

🌍 Abroad — Iraq & Afghanistan

PMCs became the visible face of the occupation. Blackwater held the State Department contract to protect U.S. diplomats in Iraq; its convoys, often firing to clear traffic, were feared by Iraqi civilians. On March 31, 2004, four Blackwater contractors were ambushed and killed and their burned bodies hung from a bridge in Fallujah — triggering the First Battle of Fallujah. On September 16, 2007, Blackwater guards opened fire in Baghdad's Nisour Square, killing 17 civilians. Coalition Provisional Authority Order 17 had granted contractors immunity from Iraqi law, so the case was ultimately tried in the U.S. under the Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (MEJA). DynCorp ran multibillion-dollar police-training programs; Triple Canopy guarded embassies. All three shared the State Department's Worldwide Protective Services (WPS) umbrella — a $10 billion ceiling in 2010, raised to $15 billion in 2020.

⚠ Scandals, Abuses & the Accountability Gap

  • Nisour Square (2007). Blackwater guards Nicholas Slatten (first-degree murder), Paul Slough, Evan Liberty and Dustin Heard (manslaughter) were convicted in 2014 — then pardoned by President Trump in December 2020, a move UN experts said undermined international humanitarian law.
  • Abu Ghraib (2003–04). Interrogators and translators from contractors CACI and Titan/L-3 were implicated in detainee abuse; in 2024 a U.S. jury found CACI liable, awarding $42 million to three former detainees.
  • DynCorp in Bosnia (late 1990s). Employees were tied to a sex-trafficking ring; whistleblower Kathryn Bolkovac was fired and won a wrongful-termination suit (the basis of the film The Whistleblower). A 2009 diplomatic cable later alleged a DynCorp-hosted party for Afghan police involving "dancing boys" (bacha bazi).
  • The accountability gap. Contractors long fell between Iraqi law (barred by CPA Order 17), the U.S. civilian courts, and the military justice system — a jurisdictional gray zone only partly closed by MEJA and later UCMJ amendments.

⚖️ "The Blackwater Four" — Action, Conviction & Pardon

The action. On September 16, 2007, a Blackwater convoy (call sign "Raven 23") halted traffic in Baghdad's crowded Nisour Square and opened fire, killing 17 Iraqi civilians and wounding more than 20. The dead included women, children, and a young medical student. Blackwater said its men responded to a perceived car-bomb threat; survivors and Iraqi investigators said the civilians were unarmed and the shooting unprovoked.

The four. Four guards — Nicholas Slatten, Paul Slough, Evan Liberty and Dustin Heard — became known as the "Blackwater Four." Because CPA Order 17 barred Iraqi prosecution, they were charged in U.S. federal court under the Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act. An initial case was dismissed in 2009 over tainted statements, then revived on appeal.

The conviction. In October 2014 a Washington, D.C. jury convicted all four: Slatten of first-degree murder, and Slough, Liberty and Heard of voluntary/attempted manslaughter and firearms counts. In 2015 Slatten received life and the other three got 30 years each (driven by a mandatory-minimum machine-gun statute). On appeal Slatten's conviction was vacated; he was retried and convicted again in 2018 (life, 2019), while the appeals court found the 30-year terms unconstitutionally harsh and the three were resentenced in 2019 to roughly 12–15 years.

The pardon. On December 22, 2020, President Donald Trump pardoned all four. The pardons were condemned by the lead prosecutor, the Iraqi government, victims' families, and U.N. human-rights experts (who said the move undermined international humanitarian law); supporters countered that the men were punished for split-second combat decisions. source ↗

🔥 Benghazi, Libya (Sept 11–12, 2012) — Diplomatic Security & the Politics of the Rescue

What happened. On the night of September 11, 2012, militants — including members of Ansar al-Sharia — assaulted the lightly-defended U.S. Special Mission in Benghazi and, hours later, the nearby CIA Annex. Four Americans died: Ambassador J. Christopher Stevens (the first U.S. ambassador killed in the line of duty since 1979) and information officer Sean Smith at the mission, and former Navy SEALs Tyrone Woods and Glen Doherty — by then CIA Global Response Staff (GRS) contractors — by mortar fire at the Annex the next morning.

The contractors on the ground. The mission's perimeter was held by a few U.S. Diplomatic Security agents, unarmed local guards from the British firm Blue Mountain Group, and a local militia. The fight and evacuation were carried out largely by the GRS contractors from the Annex — the operators whose account became the book and 2016 film 13 Hours: The Secret Soldiers of Benghazi.

The inactions — security beforehand. The State Department had repeatedly been asked to bolster security in Libya. Regional Security Officer Eric Nordstrom sent cables in March and July 2012 requesting reinforcements that were not provided. The independent Accountability Review Board (Amb. Thomas Pickering and Adm. Mike Mullen, Dec. 2012) found "systemic failures and leadership and management deficiencies at senior levels" of the State Department and security that was "grossly inadequate." Four officials were reassigned; none was found to have willfully breached duty.

The disputed "stand-down" and the rescue. GRS operators later said the CIA base chief twice told them to wait — a delay of roughly 20 minutes — before they drove to the mission, which they cast as a "stand-down order." The base chief denied giving any such order, and congressional investigations (including the Republican-led House committees) and military officers concluded there was no stand-down order that held back a rescue capable of saving lives: no armed U.S. aircraft or quick-reaction force was close enough to reach Benghazi in time, though a small team (including Doherty) flew in from Tripoli. The point remains politically contested.

The political fallout. Days later, U.N. Ambassador Susan Rice attributed the attack on Sunday talk shows to a spontaneous protest over an anti-Islam video — an account soon corrected, fueling charges the administration had misled the public. As Secretary of State, Hillary Clinton took responsibility for departmental security and testified repeatedly (her January 2013 "What difference, at this point, does it make?" exchange became a flashpoint). About ten investigations followed, culminating in the House Select Committee on Benghazi (2014–2016), whose report faulted security and the response but found no new evidence of wrongdoing by Clinton — even as the inquiry incidentally exposed her private email server. Attacker Ahmed Abu Khattala was later captured and convicted on terrorism charges. source ↗

🏠 At Home — Contractors on U.S. Soil

The privatized force did not stay overseas. After Hurricane Katrina in September 2005, Blackwater operators — some fresh from Iraq, in body armor and carrying automatic weapons — deployed to New Orleans under a Department of Homeland Security / Federal Protective Service contract worth more than $30 million; one operator claimed to have been deputized by Louisiana's governor (the state denied it). It was the first large domestic deployment of a war-zone security firm on American streets, raising posse comitatus and accountability questions. Domestically, Triple Canopy and successor Constellis have held Federal Protective Service contracts guarding U.S. government buildings and, per 2026 reporting, immigration-enforcement (ICE) work. Blackwater's 7,000-acre Moyock, North Carolina campus (opened 1998) has trained tens of thousands of U.S. police and military personnel.
